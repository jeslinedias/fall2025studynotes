Overview and Asset Recognition Criteria

What are Long-Term Assets?

Long-term assets are investments a company makes to generate profit in the coming years. They are not intended for sale to customers in the normal course of business. These assets are categorized on the balance sheet as non-current assets.

Recognition Criteria

For an item to be recognized as an asset on the balance sheet, it must meet three criteria:

  • The company must control the asset.
  • The company must expect a future economic benefit from the asset.
  • The company must be able to reliably measure the value of the asset.

Practical Examples

  • Control: A delivery truck with the company's name on it. The company holds legal title and decides how it is used.
  • Future Benefit: A factory machine that manufactures products to be sold to customers for revenue.
  • Reliable Measurement: A patent that a company purchases from an inventor for a specific price. The cost is a verifiable number.

Expense vs. Asset

The determination of whether an item is an asset or an expense depends on whether it meets the three recognition criteria above. Below are examples of both.

Examples of Assets

  • Land: This is an asset because it is controlled, provides future benefit (a place for the factory), and can be reliably measured by its purchase price.
  • Building: An asset for the same reasons as land.
  • Equipment: An asset because it provides a future benefit by creating products or services.

Examples of Expenses

  • Salaries and Wages: Paid in the current period for work already performed. They do not provide a future benefit that can be separated from the employee.
  • Utilities: Used and consumed in the current period.
  • Minor Repairs: For minor repairs and maintenance, the benefit is in the current period, so it is an expense.
  • Training Costs: These are expensed immediately because a company cannot control the employee, who may leave the company after being trained. Therefore, the future economic benefit is not certain.
  • Research and Development (R&D): R&D costs are expensed as incurred because the future economic benefit from a project is too uncertain. It's not possible to reliably measure whether a specific R&D project will lead to a marketable product or patent.
  • Marketing Costs: These are expensed immediately because the future economic benefit from a marketing campaign is uncertain and difficult to reliably measure. A company cannot control whether the marketing will lead to future sales.

Fixed Assets (PPE)

Basket Purchases

When multiple assets are acquired for a single lump-sum price, this is a basket purchase. The total cost must be allocated to each asset based on its relative fair value.

$$\text{Allocation to Asset A} = \text{Total Cost} \times \left( \frac{\text{Fair Value}_A}{\text{Total Fair Value}} \right)$$

Acquisition and Post-Acquisition Expenditures

Acquisition Costs (Capitalized)

The cost of a fixed asset includes all expenditures necessary to get the asset ready for its intended use. These costs are capitalized, meaning they are recorded on the balance sheet as part of the asset's value.

  • Purchase price
  • Taxes, duties, and tariffs
  • Freight and delivery costs
  • Installation and setup fees
  • Testing costs

Post-Acquisition Expenditures

Expenditures after an asset is acquired are classified as either an asset or an expense based on their impact. If they increase the asset's useful life or productivity, they are capitalized. If they simply maintain the asset, they are expensed.

Capitalized Expenditures

  • Major Overhauls: A complete engine replacement in a delivery truck that extends its useful life by several years.
  • Significant Upgrades: Adding new components to a machine that significantly increases its output capacity.

Expensed Expenditures

  • Routine Maintenance: Changing the oil or tires on a company vehicle.
  • Minor Repairs: Fixing a small dent or replacing a broken light bulb. These costs simply maintain the asset in its current state.

Depreciation, Depletion, and Amortization

These three terms are methods for allocating the cost of a long-term asset to expense over its useful life. They are a core application of the matching principle, which states that expenses should be recognized in the same period as the revenues they help generate.

Depreciation

This is the allocation of the cost of tangible assets, such as buildings and equipment, to expense over their useful lives. It reflects the gradual consumption of the asset's economic benefits.

Depletion

This is the allocation of the cost of natural resources, such as timber, minerals, and oil, to expense as the resource is extracted or consumed. It is a form of depreciation specific to wasting assets.

Example: A company buys a coal mine for $1,000,000 with an estimated 100,000 tons of coal. As it extracts and sells 10,000 tons, it would record a depletion expense of $100,000 (10 per ton).

Amortization

This is the allocation of the cost of intangible assets with a finite useful life, such as patents and copyrights, to expense over their useful lives. Intangible assets with indefinite useful lives, like trademarks, are not amortized.

Example: A company acquires a patent for $50,000 that has a remaining legal life of 10 years. It would record an annual amortization expense of $5,000 to spread the cost over its useful life.

Depreciation and Book Value

Straight-Line Depreciation Method

The most common method, straight-line depreciation, allocates an equal amount of expense to each period of an asset's useful life. The formula is as follows:

$$\text{Annual Depreciation Expense} = \frac{\text{Cost} - \text{Salvage Value}}{\text{Useful Life}}$$

Once you calculate the annual depreciation, you can find the asset's book value at any point in time.

Book Value

An asset's book value (also known as net book value or carrying amount) is its original cost minus all accumulated depreciation recorded to date. This is the value at which the asset is reported on the balance sheet.

$$\text{Book Value} = \text{Cost} - \text{Accumulated Depreciation}$$

Example

A company purchases a machine for $100,000 with an estimated useful life of 10 years and a salvage value of $10,000.

Annual Depreciation: $$\frac{100,000 - 10,000}{10 \text{ years}} = 9,000 \text{ per year}$$

Journal Entry for each year:

Debit: Depreciation Expense: $9,000

Credit: Accumulated Depreciation: $9,000

Book Value at the end of Year 1:

$$100,000 - 9,000 = 91,000$$

Book Value at the end of Year 2:

$$100,000 - (9,000 \times 2) = 82,000$$

At the end of its useful life (Year 10), the book value will equal the salvage value:

$$100,000 - (9,000 \times 10) = 10,000$$

Accounting for Asset Impairments

An asset is considered impaired if its book value is greater than its recoverable amount. This means the company cannot recover the full value of the asset from its future cash flows. Companies are required to perform an impairment test whenever events or changes in circumstances indicate the book value may not be recoverable.

The Two-Step Impairment Test

A two-step process is used to determine if a long-term asset is impaired and to calculate the impairment loss.

  • Step 1: Test for Impairment (Recoverability Test)

    Compare the asset's book value with the undiscounted future cash flows expected to be generated by the asset. If the undiscounted future cash flows are less than the book value, the asset is considered impaired, and you proceed to Step 2.

    If Book Value > Undiscounted Future Cash Flows, Then Impairment exists.

  • Step 2: Calculate Impairment Loss

    If the asset is impaired, the impairment loss is calculated as the difference between the book value and the asset's fair value (which is the discounted future cash flows or the market price).

    Impairment Loss = Book Value - Fair Value

Example of Impairment

A company has a machine with a book value of $200,000. The company determines that the undiscounted future cash flows from the machine are only $180,000, and its fair value is $150,000.

  • Step 1: Since the book value ($200,000) is greater than the undiscounted future cash flows ($180,000), the asset is impaired.
  • Step 2: The impairment loss is the difference between the book value and the fair value: $$\text{Loss} = 200,000 - 150,000 = 50,000$$

Journal Entry

Debit: Impairment Loss: $50,000

Credit: Accumulated Depreciation: $50,000

The debit to Impairment Loss decreases net income, and the credit to Accumulated Depreciation decreases the asset's book value on the balance sheet.

Transaction Worksheet: Impairment Loss

Here is how an impairment loss transaction is recorded and how it affects the balance sheet, income statement, and statement of cash flows.

Account Name Transaction (Debit/Credit) Balance Sheet Impact Income Statement Impact Statement of Cash Flows Impact
Accumulated Depreciation Credit $50,000 Increases by $50,000 N/A N/A
Impairment Loss Debit $50,000 N/A Increases by $50,000 N/A
Total Impact Total Assets decrease by $50,000 Net Income decreases by $50,000 No cash flow impact

Note: The accumulated depreciation account is a contra-asset account, so an increase in its balance (credit) results in a decrease in the overall asset value on the balance sheet. This is why the impairment loss, a non-cash expense, reduces both the book value of the asset and net income.

Intangible Assets

Intangible assets are long-term assets that lack physical substance. They provide a future economic benefit through legal rights or control of intellectual property. Like tangible assets, they must meet the three recognition criteria to be capitalized on the balance sheet.

Patents

A patent gives a company the exclusive right to a product or process for a certain period. It is an asset because it is controlled (legal protection), provides a future economic benefit (exclusive use and revenue generation), and its cost can be reliably measured (purchase price or legal fees).

Copyrights

A copyright provides the exclusive right to reproduce and sell an artistic or literary work. It is an asset because the company has legal control, it provides a future benefit through sales of the protected work, and the cost of obtaining the copyright can be reliably measured.

Trademarks

A trademark is an exclusive right to a name, logo, or symbol. It is an asset because the company controls the mark, it provides future economic benefit by building brand recognition and loyalty, and its cost is reliably measured by its purchase price or legal fees.

Disposal of Property, Plant, and Equipment

When a company disposes of a long-term asset, they must remove the asset from the books and recognize any resulting gain or loss. This involves removing the original cost of the asset and its accumulated depreciation from the balance sheet.

Example Journal Entry

Debit: Cash (if any received)

Debit: Accumulated Depreciation

Debit: Loss on Disposal (if applicable)

Credit: Asset (original cost)

Credit: Gain on Disposal (if applicable)

The net book value of the disposed asset is equal to the original cost minus the accumulated depreciation.

Gross vs. Net PPE

Companies can report PPE in two ways on the balance sheet, reflecting different levels of detail.

Gross PPE

Gross PPE is the total cost of all long-term assets, before subtracting any accumulated depreciation. It represents the original investment in the assets.

Net PPE

Net PPE is the total cost of assets minus accumulated depreciation. This is the book value of the assets and is the most common way to report PPE on the balance sheet.

$$\text{Net PPE} = \text{Gross PPE} - \text{Accumulated Depreciation}$$

Additions to PPE

Additions to PPE represent all new purchases of long-term assets during a period. These can be calculated using a simple reconciliation formula.

$$\text{Gross PPE}_\text{end} = \text{Gross PPE}_\text{beg} + \text{Additions} - \text{Original cost of disposed items}$$

According to the Walmart case study in the transcript, the value of additions to plant, property, and equipment in fiscal year 2022 can be calculated as follows, based on the provided data:

  • Gross PPE (end of 2022): $53,368 million
  • Gross PPE (beginning of 2022): $49,887 million
  • Original cost of disposed items: $459 million

$$\text{Additions} = \$53,368 \text{M} - \$49,887 \text{M} + \$459 \text{M} = \$3,940 \text{M}$$

Tangible Assets Example

Net PPE Reconciliation

You can reconcile the Net PPE balance from the beginning to the end of a period using a similar framework. This approach is useful when you have the net book values but not the gross balances.

To calculate the total cost of additions to PPE in a period, you need to use the following values from a company's financial statements:

  • Net PPE at the beginning of the period
  • Net PPE at the end of the period
  • Depreciation and amortization expense for the period
  • Net book value of disposed items for the period

$$\text{Additions} = (\text{Net PPE}_{\text{end}} - \text{Net PPE}_{\text{beg}}) + \text{Depreciation} + \text{Net book value of disposed items}$$

The rationale for this formula is that the change in a company's net PPE is driven by three factors: new additions, depreciation of existing assets, and the disposal of old assets. By adding back the depreciation and the net book value of disposed assets to the change in net PPE, you can isolate the value of new asset purchases, or additions.

Fixed Assets Problem Solver

The Problem

The fixed assets account of Brothers Inc. has a balance of $100,000 and $120,000 at the end of year 2025 and year 2024, respectively. The accumulated depreciation account balances are $50,000 and $60,000 at the end of year 2025 and year 2024, respectively. The depreciation expense in year 2025 is $10,000. Brother Inc. sold a truck for $25,000 in cash and purchased new tools worth $42,000 in year 2025. What is the original cost of the truck sold?

1. Original Cost of the Item Sold

The Gross PPE (Property, Plant, and Equipment) account tracks the original cost of assets. We can find the cost of the disposed asset by looking at the change in this account. The change is caused by new additions and the removal of disposed assets.

$$\text{Gross PPE}_\text{end} = \text{Gross PPE}_\text{beg} + \text{Additions} - \text{Original cost of disposed item}$$

$$\text{Original cost of disposed item} = \text{Gross PPE}_\text{beg} + \text{Additions} - \text{Gross PPE}_\text{end}$$

2. Accumulated Depreciation on the Item Sold

The Accumulated Depreciation account tracks the total depreciation recorded for all assets. The change in this account is due to the depreciation expense for the period and the removal of the accumulated depreciation related to the disposed asset. We can isolate the latter to find the accumulated depreciation on the item sold.

$$\text{Acc Dep}_\text{end} = \text{Acc Dep}_\text{beg} + \text{Depreciation Expense} - \text{Acc Dep on item sold}$$

$$\text{Acc Dep on item sold} = \text{Acc Dep}_\text{beg} + \text{Depreciation Expense} - \text{Acc Dep}_\text{end}$$

3. Gain/Loss from the Sale

A gain or loss on sale is the difference between the cash received and the asset's **book value** at the time of sale. The book value is the original cost minus its accumulated depreciation.

$$\text{Book Value} = \text{Original Cost} - \text{Accumulated Depreciation}$$

$$\text{Gain/Loss} = \text{Cash Received} - \text{Book Value}$$
