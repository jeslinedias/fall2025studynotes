Recording Transactions and Financial Statement Preparation

Introduction to Recording Financial Transactions

This module focuses on how to record financial transactions in accounting language and prepare the fundamental financial statements: the Balance Sheet and Income Statement.

Key Principle: Fundamental Accounting Equation

The entire system of recording financial transactions revolves around maintaining the balance of the equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

For any period, this also means that changes in these components must balance:

$$\Delta \text{Assets} = \Delta \text{Liabilities} + \Delta \text{Shareholders' Equity}$$

Where $\Delta$ (Delta) signifies "change."

Steps in Recording Financial Transactions:

  1. Identify accounts impacted: Determine which specific accounts (e.g., Cash, Fixed Assets, Bank Loan, Sales) are affected by a transaction.
  2. Determine the impact: Quantify the amount and direction (increase/decrease) of the impact on each identified account.
  3. Ensure the fundamental accounting equation holds: Verify that the total change in assets equals the total change in liabilities plus the total change in shareholders' equity.

The Transaction Worksheet

The transaction worksheet is a graphical representation of the fundamental accounting equation, used to record and track financial transactions.

It typically includes:

  • Opening Balance: The cumulative balance of each account at the start of the period.
  • Transactions: Individual financial events recorded as they occur.
  • Closing Balance: The sum of the opening balance and all transactions for the period.

Examples of Recording Transactions:

Example 1: Purchase a Truck

A firm purchases a truck for $65,000 with a bank loan.

Example 2: Sale of Goods

A firm sells goods (cost $1,000) for $1,500 in cash.

  • Part 1 (Sale):
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Cash (Asset) increases by $1,500
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Income Statement (Shareholders' Equity) increases by $1,500 (Revenue)
  • Part 2 (Cost of Sale):
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Inventories (Asset) decreases by ($1,000)
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Income Statement (Shareholders' Equity) decreases by ($1,000) (Cost of Goods Sold Expense)
  • Equation Check: $\Delta \text{Assets} (\$1,500 - \$1,000 = \$500) = \Delta \text{Liabilities} (\$0) + \Delta \text{Shareholders' Equity} (\$1,500 - \$1,000 = \$500)$ - Holds!

Example 3: Utility Bill Received

A firm receives a utility bill of $850 (not yet paid).

  • ‚û°Ô∏è Accounts Payable (Liability) increases by $850
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Income Statement (Shareholders' Equity) decreases by ($850) (Utility Expense)
  • Equation Check: $\Delta \text{Assets} (\$0) = \Delta \text{Liabilities} (\$850) + \Delta \text{Shareholders' Equity} (\$-850)$ - Holds!

Income Statement Mirroring on Retained Earnings

At the end of a period, the **net amount on the Income Statement column** is transferred (mirrored) to the **Retained Earnings column** (a Shareholders' Equity account). A positive net income increases retained earnings, while a net loss decreases it. This step is crucial for maintaining the fundamental accounting equation and preparing the balance sheet.

Individual Accounts Change Over Time

All individual financial accounts follow a common framework for how their balances change over a period:

$$\text{Beginning Balance} + \text{Additions during the period} - \text{Reductions during the period} = \text{Ending Balance}$$

This formula can be rearranged to find any missing component.

Example: Cash Paid to Suppliers (Accounts Payable)

If:

  • Beginning Accounts Payable: $1,000
  • Additions (new credit purchases): $10,000
  • Ending Accounts Payable: $3,000

To find the Reductions during the period (Cash paid to suppliers):

$$\$1,000 + \$10,000 - \text{X} = \$3,000$$ $$\text{X} = \$11,000 - \$3,000 = \$8,000$$

So, $8,000 was paid to suppliers.

Accrual Accounting

Accrual accounting is a fundamental concept in modern financial reporting. It aims to reflect the economic reality of a firm's performance, even when no cash is involved in a transaction.

Core Principles of Accrual Accounting:

  • Revenues are recognized when earned, regardless of when cash is received.
  • Expenses are recognized when incurred, regardless of when cash is paid.

This means that under accrual accounting, **profits are generally NOT equal to the change in cash** over a period.

Key Concepts in Accrual Accounting:

  • Accruals: Accounts like Prepaid Expenses (e.g., prepaid insurance, prepaid rent), Accounts Payable, and Accounts Receivable. These accounts track revenues earned but not yet received (receivables) or expenses incurred but not yet paid (payables), or payments made for future benefits (prepaid expenses).
  • Adjusting Entries: These are entries made at the end of an accounting period to record revenues that have been earned but not yet recorded, and expenses that have been incurred but not yet recorded. They ensure that financial statements accurately reflect the economic activities of the period.

Example: Prepaid Insurance

A company pays $1,200 for an annual insurance policy covering July 2024 to June 2025. Fiscal year ends December 31, 2024.

  • Initial Payment (July 2024):
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Cash (Current Asset) decreases by ($1,200).
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Prepaid Insurance (Current Asset) increases by $1,200.
  • Adjustment (Dec 31, 2024): 3 months of insurance have been used (July, Aug, Sept).
  • Monthly cost: $\frac{\text{\$1,200}}{\text{12 months}} = \text{\$100/month}$
  • Insurance Expense incurred: $$\text{3 months} \times \text{\$100/month} = \text{\$300}$$
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Insurance Expense (Income Statement/Shareholders' Equity) decreases by ($300).
  • ‚û°Ô∏è Prepaid Insurance (Current Asset) decreases by ($300).
  • The remaining $900 in Prepaid Insurance (for 9 months in 2025) remains an asset.

Accrual Accounting vs. Cash Accounting

  • Cash Accounting: Simple, but doesn't reflect economic reality (e.g., sales are only recorded when cash is received).
  • Accrual Accounting: Reflects economic reality, but requires managerial judgment (e.g., estimating expenses or recognizing revenue before cash changes hands), which can potentially lead to earnings manipulation.
