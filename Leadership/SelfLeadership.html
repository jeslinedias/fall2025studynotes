Lesson 2-1: Identifying as a Leader

This section explores the fundamental concept of leader identity – how individuals perceive themselves as leaders – and why this self-perception is crucial for leadership development. It emphasizes that leadership is not just a role, but a deeply ingrained identity.

What is Leader Identity?

Leader identity is the extent to which someone perceives themselves to be a leader. It's a sub-component of one's overall identity that relates to being a leader or how one thinks of oneself as a leader. It's both cognitively and socially constructed, influenced by our relationships and how others react to us.

Leader Identity Assessment (Self-Report Survey)

Reflect on the following statements to assess your current leader identity:

  • I am a leader.
  • I see myself as a leader.
  • If I had to describe myself to others, I would include the word leader.
  • I prefer being seen by others as a leader.

Why Leader Identity Matters: Building Leader Capacity

Your belief in yourself as a leader is the starting point for effective leadership. Leadership develops through a ready, willing, and able reciprocal process, with leader identity and self-efficacy being the initial threshold.

Lesson 2-2: Developing Self-awareness

Self-awareness is often considered the most fundamental skill for effective and sustained leadership. It involves a deep understanding of ourselves and how others perceive us, enabling us to differentiate our internal 'noise' from external signals.

The Power of Self-Awareness

Connecting deeply with others in trusting leader-follower relationships requires accurate self-awareness. However, studies show only 10-15% of people truly fit the criteria for being self-aware. It is about knowing ourselves inside and out, and being willing to be vulnerable.

Benefits of High Self-Awareness

  • More confident and creative
  • Make sounder decisions
  • Build stronger relationships
  • Communicate more effective
  • Less likely to lie, cheat, and steal
  • Better workers, more promotions
  • More effective leaders with more satisfied employees and more profitable companies

Internal vs. External Self-Awareness (Tasha Eurich's Framework)

Self-awareness has two dimensions, and they are largely unrelated. Developing as leaders requires focusing on both.

Developing Self-Awareness: Gain Insight & Invite Feedback

To overcome personal blind spots and enhance self-awareness, leaders must actively engage in two key practices:

1. Gaining Insight (Internal Self-Awareness)

Carve out quiet time to explore these seven areas for yourself:

  • Values: Core principles guiding decisions.
  • Passions: Projects/activities you never tire of.
  • Aspirations: Experiences/achievements you seek; legacy.
  • Fit/Environment: Ideal work environment for happiness and engagement.
  • Patterns: Your personality (e.g., from assessments).
  • Reactions: Top strengths and weaknesses; how you respond.
  • Impact: Effect you have on others, one-on-one and in groups.

2. Inviting Feedback (External Self-Awareness)

The most successful leaders seek frequent critical feedback, especially from "loving critics" – people who have your best interests in mind and are willing to tell you the truth. Respond non-defensively and celebrate their courage.

"It's much better to know that you're going to make mistakes and invite individuals to share with you when you've done something that represents the wrong way."

Lesson 2-3: Making Effective Decisions

Leaders make countless decisions daily, impacting many. This section delves into decision-making processes, common biases, and strategies for improving judgment and choice. Understanding how our brains work is the first step to making better decisions.

Decision-Making: Processes and Theories

Decision-making involves two primary processes: judgment or evaluation (identifying alternatives) and choice or selection (selecting among them).

Historical Views: Rationality

  • Descartes' Square: A simple yet powerful technique developed by philosopher René Descartes to thoroughly evaluate any option. It involves asking four key questions to gain a comprehensive and objective understanding of a situation, helping to clarify consequences and make more informed decisions. Learn more by watching this video: How to Make a Decision You Won't Regret Later
    • What will happen if I do this?
    • What will happen if I don't do this?
    • What won't happen if I do this?
    • What won't happen if I don't do this?
  • Game Theory: A theoretical framework for conceiving social situations among competing players. It's often applied in economics, political science, and psychology to understand how rational decision-makers interact and make choices when their outcomes depend on the choices of others.
  • Rational Choice Theory / Expected Value Theory: This theory assumes that individuals make decisions that are logical and linear, always aiming to maximize their personal utility or gain. It posits that people weigh the costs and benefits of different options and choose the one that offers the greatest net benefit.

Modern Views: Bounded Rationality & Biases

  • Bounded Rationality (Herbert Simon): This concept argues that human rationality is limited by cognitive constraints, available information, and the finite amount of time we have to make decisions. Instead of perfectly rational choices, individuals often "satisfice"—they seek an outcome that is good enough, rather than searching for the absolute best possible outcome.
  • Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky): This theory describes how individuals make decisions under risk and uncertainty. A key finding is that people tend to value losses more heavily than equivalent gains, leading to different risk preferences depending on whether a decision is "framed" as a gain or a loss. For example, people are often more risk-averse when choosing between sure gains but more risk-seeking when choosing between sure losses.

Two Decision-Making Systems (Kahneman)

  • System 1 (Fast & Reactive): This system operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control. It's based on experience and uses heuristics (mental shortcuts) which can lead to cognitive biases. (e.g., tying shoes, checking email).
  • System 2 (Slower & Deliberate): This system allocates attention to effortful mental activities that demand it, including complex computations. It involves taking time to gather information, analyzing it, and using decision tools, which can help in overcoming biases and making more rational decisions. (e.g., analytical decision-making).

Skill Development: Making Better Decisions

Improving decision-making starts with self-awareness and proactive strategies:

Know Yourself

Understand your values, goals, and individual differences that influence decisions (e.g., fear of disappointing others, desire to be liked).

Recognize Blind Spots

Be aware of external factors like time and social constraints that affect your judgment.

Utilize Social Networks

Avoid making decisions in a vacuum. Consult "loving critics" and diverse perspectives to gain full information.

Develop a Problem-Solving Practice

Create templates or decision trees to structure your thinking and remind you to avoid quick heuristics.

The PDCA Learning Cycle for Continuous Improvement

Apply the Plan-Do-Check-Adjust (PDCA) cycle to your leadership practice:

  • Plan: Decide what you want to improve (e.g., humble inquiry in meetings).
  • Do: Practice the new behavior.
  • Check: Reflect on how you did (e.g., at the end of the week).
  • Adjust: Adapt your approach for the next iteration.

Ultimately, leaders must own up to the tough decisions they make and foster a team culture built on trusting relationships (bonding and bridging social capital) to facilitate cooperation and shared motivation.

Lesson 2-4: Making Ethical Decisions

Ethical decision-making is critical for leaders, as their choices profoundly impact individuals and organizations. This section explores why well-intentioned people can make unethical decisions and provides a framework for avoiding common pitfalls.

Understanding Ethical Decision-Making

Ethics refers to evaluating decisions or behaviors as good vs. bad or right vs. wrong. Ethical decision-making specifically examines how we make choices aligned with (or against) ethical principles, influenced by personal and cultural values.

Normative Ethics (Philosophical Frameworks)

  • Utilitarianism: This framework focuses on outcomes, suggesting that the most ethical choice is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number of people.
  • Deontology: This approach emphasizes duties or rules, asserting that actions are right or wrong based on whether they adhere to a set of moral principles or rules, regardless of the outcome.
  • Virtue Ethics: Instead of focusing on actions or outcomes, virtue ethics centers on the character of the moral agent. It suggests that ethical decisions arise from developing and exercising virtues like honesty, courage, and compassion.

Behavioral Ethics (Why We Make Unethical Decisions)

  • Explores individual and situational forces, answering: Why do people make unethical decisions even when they don't intend to? It focuses on well-intentioned people making bad decisions.

Key Findings from Behavioral Ethics

  • Values vs. Actions: The Slippery Slope Effect

    This highlights a disconnect between a person's espoused values and their actual behavior. People often rationalize minor ethical violations, which can lead to a "slippery slope" where larger ethical breaches become easier and more normalized. Being explicit about values can disrupt this.

    Example: A slight inflation of expense reports or taking office supplies for personal use might seem insignificant initially, but can lead to a gradual erosion of ethical boundaries, making larger ethical lapses more likely.

  • Bounded Ethicality: Constraints on Moral Decision-Making

    Our ability to make optimal ethical decisions is limited by internal psychological biases and external situational pressures. We're not always as rational or ethical as we perceive ourselves to be.

    • Internal Psychological Biases:
      • Overconfidence Bias: Believing one is more ethical than they are, or immune to ethical pitfalls.
      • Confirmation Bias: Seeking and interpreting information that confirms existing, potentially ethically questionable, beliefs.
      • Self-Serving Bias: Attributing successes to oneself and failures to external factors, which can rationalize unethical behavior.
    • External Situational Pressures:
      • Group/Society Pressure: Conforming to group norms even if they are ethically ambiguous.
      • Incentive Structures: Reward systems that inadvertently encourage unethical behavior (e.g., aggressive sales quotas leading to unauthorized account creation, as seen in the Wells Fargo scandal).
      • Authority Pressure: Complying with unethical directives from superiors.
  • Framing Effects: How Presentation Shapes Ethical Choices

    The way a situation is presented can significantly alter ethical decisions. A "business frame" can blind decision-makers to moral implications.

    Example: The **Ford Pinto case**, where Ford conducted a cost-benefit analysis and chose not to implement an $11 safety fix, valuing human lives in monetary terms. This was a decision framed primarily as a business problem rather rather than an ethical one, leading to numerous fatalities when gas tanks exploded upon impact.

Lesson 2-5: Maintaining Psychological Well-Being

Leadership is demanding, often leading to stress and burnout. This section emphasizes the crucial importance of psychological well-being for leaders, defining its dimensions and offering practical strategies for maintaining it as a sustainable lifestyle.

Why Leader Well-Being Matters

Leadership roles often come with immense pressure, demanding long hours, complex decision-making, and constant interaction. Without intentional practices for well-being, leaders can experience high levels of stress, burnout, and a decline in effectiveness. Prioritizing psychological well-being allows leaders to sustain their energy, make better decisions, foster healthier team environments, and ultimately, thrive.

Common Well-Being Traps for Leaders:

Leaders, despite their best intentions, can sometimes fall into patterns that inadvertently harm their well-being. Recognizing these "traps" is the first step toward avoiding them:

  • The Positivity Trap

    This occurs when leaders feel pressured to always be positive, to never show weakness, or to suppress challenging emotions. As Denise Loyd notes in the transcript, "For me that stands out is the positivity trap... we can put a lot of pressure on ourselves and have even higher expectations... Sometimes than those around us and be holding ourselves to these high standards." This constant pressure to project positivity can lead to emotional exhaustion and a disconnection from one's authentic feelings.

  • The "Have All The Answers" Trap

    Leaders can place undue pressure on themselves to know everything and solve every problem independently. Denise Loyd further explains, "and have all the answers trap. So as leaders, we can put a lot of pressure on ourselves... Versus recognizing that a big part of it is bringing together the team, right? The individuals that you, that you engage with, right us, that support each other, that inform that you know, have our backs." This trap can shut down collaboration, prevent seeking help, and lead to isolation and immense personal burden.

Skill Development: Cultivating Well-Being

Maintaining psychological well-being is a lifestyle, not just crisis management. Focus on two main strategies:

1. Maximize the Positive

Intentionally cultivate what matters to your well-being. The PERMA Model (Martin Seligman) offers a framework for flourishing:

  • Positive Emotions: Seek moments of joy and pleasure.
  • Engagement: Find flow in activities you enjoy.
  • Relationships: Build supportive connections. (As Kari Keating emphasizes in the transcript, "Connection is so important." Elizabeth Luckman also adds, "it is a mutual admiration society and a mutual support society and I'm grateful for it every day.")
  • Meaning: Explore purpose and significance in life and work (coherence, significance).
  • Achievement: Pursue accomplishments that bring satisfaction.

2. Navigate the Negatives

This involves setting realistic boundaries and practicing self-compassion. Know what to say "yes" and "no" to. Remember the "oxygen mask" analogy: self-care is a continuous practice, not just for crises. Embrace "emotional agility" – being flexible with thoughts and feelings.

  • Connection: Seek support from others instead of isolating.
  • Contribution: Gain a sense of agency by contributing to initiatives or helping others.
  • Compassion: Be patient and kind to yourself and to others; accept the full range of emotions.

Ultimately, leaders must own up to the tough decisions they make and foster a team culture built on trusting relationships (bonding and bridging social capital) to facilitate cooperation and shared motivation.

Conclusion: Leadership as a Lifelong Journey

As we wrap up our exploration of strategic self-leadership, it's essential to remember that leadership is a continuous process of learning, growth, and self-improvement. It's a field of study and practice, much like any other discipline, where skills are honed over a lifetime.

Key Takeaways from Your Leadership Journey:

  • Leadership is a Learnable Skill: As highlighted in Module 1, leadership is not an innate trait but a legitimate discipline with skill-based practices, much like math or music. You can learn the skills and techniques over the course of your lifetime. Denise Loyd encourages: "I really hope that our students already see that this is something that can be learned, right? That we can learn and we can grow and keeping that mindset of growing, it's just critical to."
  • Embrace a Growth Mindset: Central to developing as a leader is the adoption of a growth mindset – the belief that abilities can be developed through dedication and hard work. Elizabeth Luckman emphasizes, "This is the growth mindset module. Everybody get in a growth mindset." This mindset encourages reflection on practices and continuous improvement.
  • Prioritize Self-Awareness: Understanding your internal thoughts and external perceptions is foundational. Pausing to reflect on your practices and how you can improve them is a great place to start.
  • Make Ethical and Effective Decisions: Recognizing and mitigating cognitive biases and understanding ethical frameworks are crucial for sound judgment. Avoid "career tragedies" by understanding common pitfalls like temptation, rationalization, and pressure.
  • Maintain Psychological Well-Being: Leadership demands self-care. It’s about maximizing positive experiences (PERMA) and navigating negatives, remembering that connection and mutual support are vital. Elizabeth Luckman shares, "I will say that the two of you are foundational to my wellbeing... it is unique to work with people and to work with people who it's not just trust, it's like beyond trust. And it is a mutual admiration society and a mutual support society and I'm grateful for it every day." Kari Keating adds, "Connection is so important."

Your journey as a leader is ongoing. Continue to reflect, learn, and grow, building on the foundations of self-awareness, ethical decision-making, and personal well-being. This approach sets you up for continued success in the act, science, and art of leadership.
